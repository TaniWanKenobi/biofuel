What Are Biofuels?

Biofuels are liquid fuels and blending components produced from biological materials called feedstocks. Most biofuels are used as transportation fuels, but they can also be used for heating and electricity generation. Think of it this way: instead of burning oil that took millions of years to form underground, biofuels burn energy from plants and organic waste that can be regrown within a single season.

The names can vary. You might see terms like biodiesel, biojet, sustainable aviation fuel, renewable diesel, or clean fuel. They all fall under the biofuels umbrella. Nearly all gasoline sold in the U.S. today is already 10% ethanol. Most people are using biofuel without even knowing it.

How It Works

The Four Main Types of Biofuels. Source: U.S. EIA, official categories under the federal Renewable Fuel Standard Program.

1. Ethanol

Ethanol is an alcohol fuel made from sugars and plant starches, most commonly corn in the U.S. and sugarcane in Brazil. The process is called fermentation. Yeast and bacteria are introduced to plant sugars, which converts the biomass into alcohol. That alcohol is then distilled and purified into fuel-grade ethanol.

  • ● Ethanol is blended into gasoline (usually 10%, called E10) to increase octane and reduce carbon monoxide emissions
  • ● It accounted for 78% of all U.S. biofuel production in 2025
  • ● In 2025, the U.S. produced 16.49 billion gallons of fuel ethanol

Step-by-Step Diagram

Harvest and Milling: The crop (sugarcane shown here, corn in the U.S.) is harvested and crushed to extract the sugar-rich juice. No alcohol yet.

Filtration (0% ethanol): The juice is filtered to remove solids and impurities before fermentation begins. The liquid at this stage contains sugar but zero alcohol.

Fermentation (0 to 10% ethanol): Yeast is added and consumes the sugars, producing ethanol as a byproduct. The mixture reaches roughly 10% ethanol, similar to wine. This takes several days.

Distillation (10 to 90% ethanol): The fermented liquid is heated. Ethanol boils at a lower temperature than water, so it evaporates first and is captured and condensed into a concentrated form, reaching around 90%.

Test and Dehydrate (99.6%+ ethanol): The ethanol is tested for purity and run through a dehydration process to remove the remaining water. Fuel-grade ethanol must be nearly pure (at least 99.6%) or it will not blend correctly with gasoline.

Mix with Gas and Ship: The finished ethanol is blended into gasoline, typically at 10% (E10), and shipped to fuel terminals. The diagram labels the end product as bio-gas, meaning the blended fuel ready for use in vehicles.

2. Biodiesel

Biodiesel is made from renewable resources like animal fats, vegetable oil, and recycled cooking grease. Through a chemical process called transesterification, those oils react with an alcohol (usually methanol) in the presence of a catalyst to produce biodiesel. It is blended with regular petroleum diesel for use in diesel engines in trucks, buses, and farm equipment.

  • ● Accounted for 5% of U.S. biofuel production in 2025 (1.15 billion gallons produced)
  • ● Rudolf Diesel, inventor of the diesel engine in 1897, actually experimented with vegetable oil as fuel

3. Renewable Diesel

Renewable diesel is chemically very similar to regular petroleum diesel, similar enough that it can be used as a straight drop-in fuel with no blending required, in any diesel engine. It accounted for about 14% of U.S. biofuel production in 2025 (2.92 billion gallons), up from just 1.50 billion gallons in 2022.

4. Other and Emerging Biofuels

This category includes:

  • ● Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF): renewable jet fuel being developed to decarbonize air travel
  • ● Renewable heating oil: used in home and building heating systems
  • ● Renewable naphtha and gasoline: still in early stages of commercialization
  • ● Biogas: methane captured from landfills, sewage plants, and livestock manure, burned for heat or electricity

These fuels accounted for about 0.62 billion gallons of U.S. production in 2025, up from 0.20 billion in 2022, and are in various stages of development.

Next Generation: Cellulosic Ethanol

Trees and grasses require far less fertilizer and water than corn and can grow on land unsuitable for food crops. Ethanol made from these sources is called cellulosic ethanol and is considered an advanced biofuel. However, it is not yet economically viable at commercial scale. As of the end of 2022, the U.S. had no commercial cellulosic ethanol production.

Advantages

"Biofuel crops grow back every season. Oil takes millions of years."

✓

Cleaner Burning

Ethanol-gasoline blends burn cleaner than pure gasoline and produce higher octane, which reduces engine knocking and cuts carbon monoxide emissions.

✓

Lower Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Relative to the fossil fuels they displace, greenhouse gas emissions are reduced by about 12% from ethanol and 41% from biodiesel. Biodiesel's advantage comes from lower agricultural inputs and more efficient conversion of feedstocks.

✓

Renewable and Regrowable

Unlike oil that takes millions of years to form, biofuel crops grow back every season. The plants also absorb CO2 while growing, making the overall carbon cycle much closer to neutral than burning fossil fuels.

✓

Safe if Spilled

Pure ethanol and biodiesel are nontoxic and biodegradable. If spilled, they break down into harmless substances, unlike petroleum, which can contaminate groundwater for decades.

✓

Energy Independence

Countries can grow their own fuel rather than importing oil from foreign nations. This is a major national security and economic benefit. The U.S. was a net exporter of biofuels in 2022, meaning it produced more than it used.

✓

Works in Existing Vehicles

Low-blend biofuels like E10 gasoline require zero modifications to existing car engines. Most drivers are already using it at every fill-up.

✓

Supports Farmers and Rural Economies

Demand for corn, soybeans, and sugarcane for biofuel creates income for agricultural communities and rural regions.

✓

Policy-Backed Growth

The federal Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS) program and California's Low Carbon Fuel Standard (LCFS) have driven consistent growth in biofuel use. Oregon and Washington have similar programs. A $1.00 per gallon tax credit for biodiesel and renewable diesel blenders is currently in effect.

Disadvantages

⚠

Food vs. Fuel

Using corn and soybeans for fuel competes with their use as food. Even if the U.S. dedicated its entire corn and soybean crop to biofuels, it would only meet about 12% of gasoline demand and 6% of diesel demand, while leaving nothing for food production.

⚠

Land Use and Deforestation

Clearing forests or natural land to grow biofuel crops releases large amounts of stored CO2 into the atmosphere. This can actually make biofuels worse for the climate than fossil fuels in some cases.

⚠

Still Requires Some Fossil Fuels to Produce

The production process for ethanol and other biofuels requires a heat source, which currently involves some fossil fuel use. It is not a fully clean process from start to finish.

⚠

Evaporative Emissions and Smog

Ethanol-gasoline blends produce higher evaporative emissions from fuel tanks and pumping equipment. These contribute to ground-level ozone and smog, a trade-off for the cleaner combustion.

⚠

Cannot Replace Oil at Scale

Biofuels cannot realistically replace all petroleum without massive impacts on food supply and land use. They are a supplement, not a full replacement.

⚠

Not Profitable Without Subsidies

Historically, biofuels have required government tax credits and mandates to compete economically with cheap petroleum. Remove the subsidies and the economics weaken significantly.

⚠

Cellulosic Promise Unfulfilled

Next-generation biofuels from non-food plant matter like grasses and wood waste have been talked about for decades, but as of the end of 2022, there was still no commercial-scale cellulosic ethanol production in the U.S.

Where in the World?

Biofuel production is concentrated in a small number of countries with strong agricultural sectors and supportive government policies. In 2024, the U.S., Brazil, and EU together accounted for nearly three-quarters of all biofuels consumed globally.

Country Spotlights

United States #1 Producer

The U.S. produces about 38% of all biofuels globally. The heart of production is the Corn Belt in Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, Nebraska, and Minnesota, where hundreds of ethanol plants process billions of bushels of corn annually. The U.S. introduced its Renewable Fuel Standard in 2005, mandating minimum biofuel volumes in transportation fuel. Main feedstocks are corn for ethanol and soybeans for biodiesel.

Brazil #2 Producer

Brazil is the world's most advanced biofuel economy. Its Proalcool program, launched in the 1970s after the oil crisis, made sugarcane ethanol a national priority. Brazil runs millions of flex-fuel vehicles that can run on any mix of gasoline and ethanol. Main feedstock is sugarcane.

Germany and the European Union #3 Region

Germany is Europe's leading biofuel producer. The EU focuses heavily on biodiesel made from rapeseed (canola) and used cooking oil. EU climate policy mandates drive continued growth.

Indonesia

Indonesia is a major and growing producer, focused on palm oil-based biodiesel. It has aggressive blending mandates as part of its national energy policy.

India and China Fast Growing

Both saw major production increases in 2024 (India +26%, China +30%) as national energy security and climate policies push biofuel adoption.

U.S. Regional Notes

  • → Midwest Corn Belt: highest concentration of ethanol plants
  • → Gulf Coast: major refinery and blending hub
  • → California: largest biofuel consumer, driven by LCFS policy
  • → Pacific Northwest (Oregon and Washington): state-level clean fuel programs
